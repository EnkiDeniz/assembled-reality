## 0. One Sentence

Lœgos reverse-engineers its own assembly path — loading every source, version, screenshot, conversation, and receipt that produced the product into a single box — proving the framework works by measuring itself, then offering the same lens to everyone who has ever wished they could see how their own work actually came together.

## 1. The Thesis

Lœgos is a document assembler built by Lakin.ai. It is live at loegos.com. It has boxes, sources, seeds, assemblies, and receipts. It has a Listen feature powered by ElevenLabs. It has a Declare Root function. It counts real sources, assemblies, and proof drafts. It prints receipts via GetReceipts with AI verification and seal status.

The thesis of the entire Lakin framework is that reality is assembled, that assembly is detectable, and that the assembly path is the proof — not the pitch, not the story, not the promise.

This spec proposes two moves:

**Move A — Self-Assembly:** Feed Lœgos its own history and let the product prove itself on itself. The first canonical entry in the Shape Library is the product that proposed the Shape Library.

**Move B — Retrospective Assembly:** Turn this self-proof into a feature that anyone can use. Everyone has a folder. Everyone has the messy history of how something got built. Lœgos makes the shape of that history legible for the first time — and in doing so, teaches the framework through the user's own experience.

The demo proves the product. The product becomes the feature. The feature becomes the onboarding. One move, three outputs.

## 4. Retrospective Assembly — The Feature

### 4.1 The Insight

While building the self-assembly box, a feature revealed itself: **everyone has a folder.**

Every founder has a Google Drive with 200 docs from their company's first year. Every researcher has a folder of papers, notes, and drafts from a project that became a thesis. Every team has a Slack archive, a Notion workspace, a shared drive full of the messy history of how something got built. Every person has a camera roll full of screenshots from a period of their life that mattered.

The history exists. The evidence exists. But it's flat. It's a pile, not a shape. People lived through it but they never *saw* it. They can tell you the story from memory, but they can't show you the assembly path. They can't point to which sources were load-bearing and which were noise. They can't identify the ghost operators. They can't find the moment the 4th point volunteered.

**Retrospective Assembly** is the feature that makes the shape of completed work legible for the first time.

### 4.2 How It Works

The user flow:

1. **"Build a box backward."** The user picks a project, a company, a product, a chapter of their life — anything that already happened and has evidence.
2. **Dump the folder.** Upload everything: documents, screenshots, photos, voice memos, pasted text, URLs. No curation required — just dump.
3. **Declare Root.** What was the origin? What started this? The user picks the source that was the seed of everything else.
4. **Seven reads the pile.** The AI layer scans all sources, identifies chronological sequence, detects thematic threads, maps which sources influenced which outcomes.
5. **Assembly path emerges.** Seven presents the shape: here's how this actually came together. Here are the moves. Here's where the basis extended. Here's where a ghost operator formed. Here's where someone volunteered.
6. **The user sees their own shape.** For the first time, the work they lived through has a visible geometry. They can navigate it. They can share it. They can seal it.

### 4.3 Why It's the Onboarding

The standard approach: give users a tutorial, explain the framework, hope they build their first box.

The Retrospective Assembly approach: **"Before you build your first box forward, build one backward."**

Pick a project you already finished. Pick a chapter that already closed. Show us the folder. We'll show you the shape.

This is inception as onboarding. The user doesn't learn the framework from a tutorial — they learn it by seeing their own history through the framework's lens. The concepts (assembly index, ghost operators, the volunteer, receipt chains) aren't taught — they're recognized. The user sees them in their own evidence and says: *that's what happened.*

The idea volunteers from within their own experience. No pitch needed.

### 4.4 Why People Will Want This

The emotional hook is not productivity. It's legibility.

People don't just want to organize their files. They want to **see what they built.** They want to understand how they got from chaos to something real. They want to point at the exact moment things changed, the exact conversation that mattered, the exact decision that was invisible at the time but turned out to be everything.

The common experience: "I wish I had documented this from the beginning." Retrospective Assembly says: you don't need to have documented it from the beginning. You have the evidence. You just didn't have the lens. Now you do.

Specific use cases:

- **Founders:** "Show me how my company actually assembled. Not the pitch deck version — the real version. The WhatsApp messages, the rejected prototypes, the midnight commits, the moment someone said yes."
- **Researchers:** "Show me how this paper actually came together. Which dead ends were necessary? Which sources were load-bearing? Where did the thesis actually form?"
- **Teams:** "Show me how this project actually shipped. Not the retrospective slide — the real assembly path. Where were the ghost operators? What was the stated process vs. the actual process?"
- **Personal:** "Show me how I got through that year. The notes I wrote, the screenshots I saved, the voice memos at 2am. What shape did it make?"

### 4.5 The Conversion Path

Retrospective Assembly → Forward Assembly → Ongoing Use

1. User builds a retrospective box (free? limited? first box free?)
2. User sees the shape of their own history
3. User understands the framework through their own evidence
4. User thinks: "What if I did this going forward? What if I captured the assembly *while it's happening*?"
5. User starts building boxes forward — now with Declare Root, Sources, Seeds, Assemblies, Receipts as a live workflow
6. User is now inside the product, using it the way it was designed to be used, because they already understand it through their own experience

The retrospective box is the bridge from "interesting concept" to "this is how I work now."

## 5. The Dennett Connection

Daniel Dennett's *From Bacteria to Bach and Back* argues that competence precedes comprehension — that complex systems can be built entirely through blind selection, from bacterial chemotaxis to Bach's fugues. Memes replicate through cultural selection. Consciousness is a user illusion. No volunteer is needed. No subject enters the structure.

The Lœgos self-assembly box is a direct test of this claim.

If Dennett is right, the assembly path of Lœgos should look like blind competence — accumulated accidents, cultural replication, no subject entering the structure. The product should be explainable without a volunteer. Every move should be reducible to selection pressure.

If the Lakin framework is right, the assembly path should show something Dennett's model can't account for: **moments where the 4th point volunteered.** The Builders saying "that would be fucking amazing" — recognition, not selection. Deniz hearing the ElevenLabs voice and realizing the product had changed — aesthetic judgment, not random variation. The decision to keep data local after seeing competitors store everything — a value entering the structure, not a fitness advantage.

The self-assembly box makes this testable. Load the evidence. Read the path. See whether the assembly is planar (Dennett: competence without comprehension, all the way up) or non-coplanar (Lakin: at some point a subject enters the structure and lifts it out of the plane).

Retrospective Assembly gives this test to everyone. Every user's box becomes a small experiment: was this project assembled by blind competence, or did something volunteer?

In Christopher Nolan's *Inception*, you cannot tell someone an idea and have it take root. You have to descend through layers until the idea volunteers from within the subject's own architecture. The inception only works if the subject generates the thought as if it originated from themselves.

**Level 3 — Forward Assembly:** The user starts building boxes going forward, capturing assembly as it happens. Now they're inside the framework, using it natively, because the inception already happened through their own retrospective.

Three layers deep. Each layer is the inception for the next.

**"When we say" = conception.** The form is already there. The statement exists.

Retrospective Assembly lives in the gap between "if" and "when." The user's own evidence is the dream architecture. The framework is the seed. The volunteer happens — or it doesn't. If it does, they're in. If it doesn't, no harm done. The box still showed them their shape.

**Lœgos as a name:** Logos (the word-structure) + Ego (the subject who speaks it). The fusion. The moment structure gains a subject. Inception to conception. The product name carries the thesis: you cannot separate the structure from the subject who assembled it.

## 9. Technical Requirements

### 9.1 Self-Assembly Box Requirements

**Source ingestion:**
- ~50–100 text documents (Apple Notes exports, framework documents, markdown files)
- ~20–30 image files (screenshots, WhatsApp captures, competitor analysis)
- ~1 GitHub repository (commit history, code snapshots, branch data)
- ~5–10 sealed receipts cross-referenced from GetReceipts
- URL sources (assembledreality.com, loegos.com, getreceipts.com)

**Seven capabilities needed:**
- Read across all sources in the box
- Identify chronological sequence (assembly path)
- Map which sources influenced which product features
- Detect ghost operators (unstated rules that governed decisions)
- Compute assembly metrics (number of distinct moves, basis extensions)
- Present the assembly as a navigable narrative with evidence links

**Receipt integration:**
- Display sealed GetReceipts entries inline with the assembly path
- Allow new receipts to be created from within the box
- Show receipt levels (L1–L5) and verification status
- The self-assembly box itself is sealable

**Listen integration:**
- Play the assembly narrative via ElevenLabs
- Block-level navigation (PREV / PLAY / NEXT)
- Speed adjustment (1x, 1.5x, 2x)
- "Seven" voice for the self-assembly narrative

### 9.2 Retrospective Assembly Feature Requirements

**Bulk source upload:**
- Drag-and-drop folder upload (or multi-file select)
- Accept mixed media: documents, images, voice memos, text pastes, URLs
- No curation required — dump first, shape later

**Auto-sequencing:**
- Seven reads all sources and proposes a chronological assembly path
- User can confirm, adjust, or override the proposed sequence
- Timestamped sources are auto-ordered; undated sources are placed by Seven's content analysis

**Shape detection:**
- Seven identifies load-bearing sources vs. peripheral ones
- Ghost operator detection: where did unstated rules form? What was the gap between stated intent and actual behavior?
- Volunteer detection: where did external validation enter? Where did someone recognize the shape?
- Assembly index computation: how many distinct, non-trivial moves produced the final state?

**Output:**
- Navigable assembly path (not a timeline — a shape)
- Listenable narrative (via Listen feature)
- Shareable box (link or embed)
- Sealable as a receipt

**Onboarding integration:**
- First-time users are prompted: "Have a project you already finished? Build your first box backward."
- Template or guided flow for retrospective assembly
- The Lœgos self-assembly box is available as a reference example
